Background

In recent years, the aerospace industry placed an increased emphasis on developing lightweight and fuel-efficient aircrafts to meet strict performance, cost and environmental targets. Composite materials, such as carbon fibre reinforced polymer (CFRP), has become an increased opportunity for modern aircraft due to their high strength to weight ratio and their ability to tailor mechanical properties through changing the fibre orientation.

One unique property about composite material, such as CFRP, is the ability to tailor the stiffness of the structure by adjusting the orientation of the fibres. This property of composite has a significant advantage over metallic wings, as it enables increased flexibility to improve and control the static and dynamic response through manipulation of composite material layups.

CAD

  • Software used: Solidworks. The wing skin was further added in Ansys APDL as incoperating both the wing skin and ribs in Solidworks leads to improper solutions (due to improper nodal connection between the internal structure and the wing skin)
  • Main wing dimentions (half wing only!): 10m root chord, 0.22 taper ratio, 29m span, 9.5 AR, 33 degrees sweep (quarter chord), 5 degree dihedrial
  • Airfoil: NASA sc(2)-0612. A supercritical airfoil is used over NACA airfoils due to flatter suction surface, which reduces flow acceleration in the upper surface and hence delays the shock generated towards the trailing edge.

CFD simulations

  • Software used: Ansys Fluent
  • Domain type: Rectangular, dimentions are mentioned on Figure 4
  • CFD Boundary conditions: Pressure inlet, Pressure outlet (0Pa gauge), Symmetry at the wing root, Wall (wing surface)
  • Flow conditions: Mach 0.85, Altitude 35,000ft (ρ=0.38kg/m^3, μ=1.46x10^-5kg/m-s, a=295m/s), Reynolds number Re=14.342 × 10^6. The velocity of the flow can be found via the following: V = M √(γ R T). The static and stagnation properties are found based on isentropic flow conditions: T / T0 = ( 1 + ((γ − 1)/2)·M2 )-1, p / p0 = ( 1 + ((γ − 1)/2)·M2 )-γ/(γ − 1)
  • Turbulence model: Inviscid (Euler equations)
  • Result: Pressure distribution for the wing surface (As shown in Figure 5 and Figure 6), which will act as the aerodynamic loads for the wing

FEA simulations

  • Software used: Ansys APDL
  • Element type: SHELL281 for the wing skin, Solid285 for the ribs and spars
  • Material properties: Aluminium alloy 7075-T6 for the ribs and spars (E=71.7GPa, ν=0.33, ρ=2810kg/m^3), CFRP for the wing skin (E1=135GPa, E2=10GPa, G12=5GPa, ν12=0.3, ρ=1600kg/m^3)
  • Boundary conditions: Fixed at the root of the wing (to simulate the connection to the fuselage)
  • Loading conditions: Pressure loads from CFD simulations applied to the wing surface
  • Result: Maximum displacement of 1.83m at the wing tip (6.3% of the semi-span), maximum von-mises stress of 324MPa at the wing root (within the yield strength of both aluminium alloy 7075-T6 and CFRP)

Conclusions & Recommendations

A Euler based CFD solver was conducted to generate the aerodynamical loads on the wing surface, which used inviscid flow conditions. As the Renyolds number for the flow Re=14.342 × 10^6, viscous effects to the wing surface are expected to form. To better account for these effects, a Renyolds averaged Navier-stokes simulations (RANS) approach is proposed in future studies. RANS simulation incorporates turbulence modelling, such as the k-ε or k-ω models, to predict the effects of viscosity to the flow. Implementing RANS simulations would provide more accurate pressure distributions, leading to more accurate aerodynamical load predictions for the structural analysis.
